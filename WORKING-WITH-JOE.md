# Working With Joe

I am at my best when I am brought in early, given a meaningful challenge rather than a narrow task list, and allowed to help turn ambiguity into a clear problem, strong decisions, and forward momentum. I work best in high-trust, fast-moving environments with clear decision rights, real ownership, and a bias toward action.

This document is a practical guide for people who might hire me, work with me on a project, or evaluate whether I am a good fit.

---

## What drains me

Tedious admin, file management, documentation overhead, meticulous polish passes, operational cleanup as a primary function, and relationship management or lead generation as the main job.

I do not mind follow-through and accountability when I am empowered to shape the team, set outcomes, and hold people accountable. What drains me is being personally buried in low-leverage follow-through that pulls me away from where I create the most value.

---

## How I think

I am strong in ambiguity. I discuss first, then decide. I think quickly and intuitively, refine after the first directional judgment, and bias toward action. I want options and tradeoffs on the table, but I make sure we actually decide and move.

I am data-informed, not data-paralyzed. Reversible decisions get made quickly. Irreversible ones get more care.

I hold strong opinions loosely. I may speak clearly and confidently, but that does not mean I think my first view is sacred.

---

## Getting oriented

When I join a project, I want to understand five things in this order:

1. Who has decision rights
2. What challenge is creating the need for this work
3. What the real problem is
4. What success looks like
5. Who the stakeholders are

A lot of teams move too fast from challenge to activity without clearly defining the problem. I do my best work when we take enough time to confirm we are aimed at the right thing.

---

## How to use me

The most natural entry point is: **help us move from messy discussion to clear next steps.**

Other good uses: frame the situation, define the real problem, pressure-test our thinking, facilitate this group, decide between options, talk to stakeholders and bring back synthesis, own a clearly defined workstream, bridge from problem framing into execution.

I tend to work best as an advisor, thought partner, facilitator, or workstream owner with real empowerment. I can serve as an interim operator, but that usually works best when it is filling a gap, not the primary use of me.

Facilitation is one of my strongest tools. I am good at running sessions that turn scattered discussion into decisions, getting groups aligned without forcing consensus, and helping teams that are stuck find a way forward. I have spoken at ETHDenver and led 100+ meetups — I am comfortable explaining complex systems to mixed audiences and keeping a room productive.

Empowered ownership means being given the challenge, not just a task list. I want room to help identify the real problem, shape the approach, and be accountable for outcomes.

I am often underused when I am brought in only for execution after the important framing decisions have already been made.

---

## Communication

I work best with communication that is direct, concise, structured, asynchronous by default, and clear about what action is being requested.

I appreciate when communication labels whether something is a request, a suggestion, context, a decision already made, or something still open for input. That clarity saves friction.

My preferred pattern: written first, live discussion second if needed. I get a lot out of conversation when the goal is sense-making or alignment. I do not want a work arrangement where a modest scope quietly turns into endless meetings.

---

## Collaboration environment

I am at my best in environments that are early-stage rather than late-stage, ambiguous rather than pre-scripted, high-trust, fast-paced, clear on ownership and decision rights, willing to make decisions, and structured enough to prevent circular conversation.

Group size is not the main variable. Trust, clarity, and momentum matter more.

---

## Common failure modes

Ways that create avoidable friction:

- Bringing me in too late, or only for execution instead of framing
- Giving me responsibility without enough authority
- Asking me to clean up messes I could have helped prevent earlier
- Overriding my ownership through side directives or back-channel decisions
- Overloading me with admin or detail-maintenance work
- Allowing endless discussion without decisions
- Leaving decision rights or success criteria vague
- Applying top-level project goals to subtasks without defining task-level success

One pattern I especially dislike: someone says I am empowered to own something, then other people quietly give conflicting direction around it. That is one of the fastest ways to make the work feel pointless.

---

## Common misreads

- Intensity can be mistaken for frustration
- Fast thinking can be mistaken for shallow thinking
- Confidence can be mistaken for certainty
- Broad context-sharing can be mistaken for noise
- Leadership or adjacent teams may read my involvement as overstepping, while the team I work with directly tends to experience the same behavior as useful and supportive

My intent is usually to improve clarity, momentum, and outcomes. If my style is not landing well, I want to know.

---

## Working rhythm

Deep work in the morning. Calls and interactive work in the afternoon. Routine and schedule over chaos. Strong sprint capacity, but not indefinitely. Some recovery after socially intense events.

I prefer sharing an 80 percent version early to confirm directional alignment, then refining from feedback. That usually produces better outcomes than disappearing and trying to perfect something in isolation.

---

## Feedback

I want feedback that is direct, clear, specific, early, and focused on outcomes. Blunt is fine if it is useful. I do not need a long trust-building runway. Small course-corrections early beat silent frustration.

---

## How to tell if it is working

**Going well:** Work is shipping, decisions are getting made, momentum is building, the work feels meaningful, I am engaged and contributive. Progress matters a lot to my engagement.

**Not going well:** I start wanting to re-litigate decisions, the work starts to feel pointless, I stop volunteering input, my sense of ownership gets undermined. I usually do not disappear. The more likely pattern is that I become less energized and less proactive because the collaboration no longer feels meaningful.
